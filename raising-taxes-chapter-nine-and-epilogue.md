# Raising Taxes

*A Discworld novel written by Claude Fable 5, with the utmost respect to Sir Terry Pratchett*

---

## Chapter Nine

*In which an arrest is effected punctually – The trial of the century, by weight – The politest sound ever made by the inevitable – A column is totalled and settled – Nine wreaths, and a tenth – Carried forward*

At one minute past midnight on Hogswatch morning, with the bells still rolling and the snow coming down on Wixon's Alley like the universe issuing receipts, Commander Sam Vimes knocked on the door of the Most Ancient and Honourable Guild of Assessors.

It opened at once. Albright Slope stood in the doorway in his good coat, hat on, a small valise packed at his feet, and behind him on the hall table a tea tray with two cups, one of them already poured and going cold, because he had estimated the Commander's arrival to within ninety seconds and had erred, characteristically, on the side of the books.

'Albright Slope,' said Vimes, 'I am arresting you on nine counts of murder, and I'm obliged to inform you that you have the right to remain silent, though from what I hear it's about forty years too late to start.'

'Quite so, Commander.' Slope picked up the valise. 'You'll find a folder in the side pocket — the procedural requirements for arrests effected on a public holiday. There's a form your sergeant should have brought. He hasn't. I took the liberty of completing it for him. It only wants your signature, and the date, which is now, I'm pleased to confirm, *officially* the date.' And Albright Slope stepped out into the snow and the lamplight and the first hour of his existence, and held out his wrists for the handcuffs with the air of a man at long last submitting an account for settlement.

Vimes cuffed him. It was, he would tell Sybil later, the most satisfying click of his entire career, and also somehow the saddest, and he had given up trying to get the two feelings to balance, on the grounds that he wasn't a bloody accountant.

They walked to Pseudopolis Yard through the falling snow, the Commander and the prisoner, past windows full of Hogswatch candles, and neither of them said anything for some way, until Slope remarked, conversationally:

'Nine to two, Commander. Cripslock's book had Mister Lipwig dead by Hogswatch.'

'I had a dollar on him living,' said Vimes. 'Don't tell my wife. Or him.'

'A sound position. I myself,' said Slope, 'never gamble. In my experience the house always wins, and I have spent my whole life being the house, so I can confirm the experience from both — ' he paused, delicately, on the threshold of the Watch house, ' — *sides of the ledger*.'

'In you go, sunshine,' said Vimes, not unkindly, and the law of Ankh-Morpork, which is for people, took Albright Slope inside, where it kept him warm, and fed him, and tried him in the spring.

---

The trial of Albright Slope lasted eleven days, and is still studied, and was decided, as everyone had known it would be, by the reading of the ledgers.

The Guild's lawyers — retained by certain anonymous parties who featured prominently in those ledgers and were therefore, it might be thought, not entirely disinterested — had built their entire defence on time. The books ran to four centuries. The relevant entries numbered in the hundreds of thousands. A human clerk reading them into the court record, they calculated, would die of old age somewhere around volume sixty, the jury having predeceased him around volume forty; the court would be obliged to accept summaries, and summaries can be argued with, and arguing was the one expense their clients could still afford.

The prosecution called one witness.

Dorfl took the stand on the third morning, was sworn in on the truth of his own chem, picked up Volume One of the Guild's true ledgers in hands that could crush granite and held it like a hymnal, and began to read. He read in a voice like the patience of brick, clear to the back of the public gallery, never hurrying, never tiring, never once losing his place. He read through the lunch recess, the court having discovered no statute requiring a golem to stop. He read through the night, by arrangement, to an empty courtroom and a single stenographer working in shifts, and the record shows that when the judge returned each morning Dorfl would announce the page number, *for the convenience of the court*, in a tone that the Times described as 'the politest sound ever made by the inevitable.'

He read for eight days. Somewhere around the fourth, the city understood that it was watching the punchline of a very old joke: the creature the Guild had classified as *industrial plant, ambulatory* was the engine of its destruction, and the property was reading the property rolls, and there was nothing in all the marble of the law that could make him stop, because he was doing the one thing no one in that courtroom had ever managed before.

He was entering the whole truth into the record.

On the eleventh day the jury was out for forty minutes, which the foreman later explained was thirty-five minutes of tea and five of voting. Guilty, on all counts. The judge, in the hush, asked the prisoner if he had anything to say before sentence.

Albright Slope rose, creaking, in the dock. He looked smaller than he had; existence, the gallery agreed, took it out of a man. He was silent for a moment, the great copperplate mind composing its final entry, and then he said:

'Only this, my lord. Everything I did, I did because I learned, very young, that the world runs on its records, and that whoever holds the pen holds the world. I was right. I wish it noted that I was *right* — the gentlemen in grey were good enough to confirm it. My error was narrower, and I commend it to the court, and to every clerk in this city, and they know who they are.' The old eyes found, in the gallery, a small man in mended cuffs, and held him. 'I thought that because the records run the world, the records were the *point*. Mister Sideways knew better. The records are a promise, my lord. They are the promise that the world makes to the people in it: *you happened, you mattered, you are owed.* I spent forty-one years forging that promise.' He straightened, and squared his edges, like a document presenting itself for filing. 'The figure is nine lives, my lord. I have totalled the column every morning since. I am ready to settle.'

They hanged him on a grey morning in May, and he went, as promised, cooperatively; the hangman, Mr Trooper's successor, reported that the gentleman had checked the paperwork on the scaffold, found a date transposed, corrected it in pencil, initialled the margin, and thanked everyone for their professionalism.¹

There were nine wreaths at the graves of nine reformers that morning, ordered years in advance through a holding company, paid in full, *very* good wreaths, with a card on each that read only: *In settlement.* And at the tenth grave, the new one, there was a single wreath that Slope had ordered for himself in '85 and kept current through forty-one years of subscription renewals — he had, after all, priced it long ago — and beside it, placed at dawn by a small figure who did not stay, a goblin pot, closed, the bead inside it rattling once in the wind, the account of Albright Slope carried, in the way of his people, not forgiven, never forgiven, but *forward*, which is all the mercy goblins believe in, and more, they would point out, than he extended to anyone.

## Epilogue

*In which the books close in the black – Rewards are distributed, after a fashion – A line in the Ledger, framed – Back-promotions, worked out proper – And a question concerning running water*

The Treasury of Ankh-Morpork closed its books that year, for the first time in living memory, in the black — so far in the black, in fact, that Vetinari ordered the surplus independently audited, on the grounds that no figure produced in Ankh-Morpork had ever before erred in the city's favour, and that this was therefore the most suspicious number in its history. (It checked out. Mr Sideways had done it himself. Twice. By weight.)

The rewards were distributed in the Patrician's own fashion.

Of-the-Twilight-the-Darkness was offered a knighthood and declined it, on the grounds that goblins do not kneel, but accepted, after negotiation conducted entirely in the orientation of pots, the post of Auditor-General of Ankh-Morpork, on the understanding that the title was, as he put it, 'is now meaning what is *should* mean, hey,' and that he would be smelling the city's accounts annually, in person, forever.

The Golem Trust received, by Act, the thing it had wanted all along, which was nothing — no special status, no protective classification, nothing but the single line in the General Ledger, *persons*, full stop, which Adora Belle had framed, and hung in the Trust's office, and was seen, once, on the evening of the Act's passage, to stand in front of with a cigarette burning down unsmoked, which among Dearhearts is the equivalent of weeping at the opera.

And Mr Sideways was summoned to the Oblong Office, where the Patrician of Ankh-Morpork rose to receive him — a courtesy extended, Drumknott would later confirm, to fewer than five living persons — and invited him to name his reward. Anything. The Collector-Generalship itself, if he wished it; Mr Lipwig had recommended him for it in writing.

Mr Sideways considered the matter with the care he gave all entries.

'I should like my back-promotions, my lord,' he said. 'As froze in '73. Worked out proper, with the increments, and the interest at Treasury rate, compounded. I done the sum myself, it's all in the folder.' He laid it on the desk. 'It comes to Senior Probationary Clerk, Second Class, and forty-one dollars sixty. And a new pen. The good kind, with the brass nib. And after that, my lord, with respect, I should like to be left exactly where I am, on account of someone has to keep the books on the man who keeps the books, and I have give this matter fifty-three years of thought, and it had better be me.'

'The city,' said Lord Vetinari, signing, 'is in your debt, Mr Sideways.'

'Yes, my lord,' said Mr Sideways. 'Volume ninety-one, page twelve. I'll let you know when it's settled.'

---

Which left, on a fine evening in spring, Moist von Lipwig, walking to the Palace with the receipts of a grateful city in his ledgers² and the gold suit in mothballs and the dangerous, fatal, creeping suspicion that everything was, at last, *finished* — and finding Lord Vetinari at the window of the Oblong Office, looking down at the city with his hands clasped behind his back, in the manner of a man listening to something underground.

'Ah, Mister Lipwig. Tell me. Are you sleeping well?'

'Like a baby, sir,' said Moist, and heard, too late, the trap close over the truth of it: he was. Eight hours a night. He had begun, the gods help him, noticing *wallpaper*.

'I feared as much.' Vetinari did not turn round. 'You are aware, of course, that beneath this city lies another city. And beneath that, another. The works of generations, Mister Lipwig — cisterns, culverts, conduits, the great buried plumbing of kings — all of it unmapped, unmaintained, and unowned, and all of it, I am informed by the engineers, approximately one wet spring from reminding us of its existence in the most emphatic possible terms. A million people, Mister Lipwig, and every drop they drink comes in by hope and goes out by accident. The dwarfs will not touch it, for reasons of treaty. The Guild of Plumbers and Dunnikindivers has ruled it does not exist, which I am given to understand is a professional opinion and was *very* expensive. It wants a man who can sell the city the thing it cannot see and will not thank him for. It wants vision. It wants nerve. It wants,' and here the Patrician turned, at last, and upon his face there was almost a quarter of a smile, 'a man who has run out of things to save.'

Far below the Palace, in the dark beneath the dark, in pipes that had not heard a footstep in three hundred years, something gurgled.

'Tell me, Mister Lipwig,' said Lord Vetinari. 'What do you know about running water?'
